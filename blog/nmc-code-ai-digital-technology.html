AI in Nursing: What the NMC's New Code Will Say About Technology

Published 24 July 2026 · Updated 24 July 2026

For the first time, the NMC's professional Code is set to address artificial intelligence and digital technology directly. The evidence review that informed the new draft Code found that emerging technologies were "the most urgent issue" for the regulator to address.

The NMC news release from 21 July 2026 states that the new Code will include standards on the "safe and effective use of digital and other technologies such as artificial intelligence (AI)." The regulator also plans new guidance on using technology effectively.

What is proposed: The new Code will include specific standards on safe and effective use of digital tools and AI. This is a direct response to the evidence review, which found both the public and professionals see benefits but are concerned about loss of clinical judgment.

Why now

The NMC conducted a year-long evidence review that included a survey with 13,757 responses from professionals and the public. The findings on technology were clear. Both groups recognised the potential of AI and digital tools to improve care, but they shared real concerns about losing the human element of nursing judgment.

Professionals told the NMC they wanted clearer standards on what counts as safe and effective use of technology. The public wanted reassurance that technology would not replace the clinical reasoning and compassion of registered professionals.

Source: NMC official news release, 21 July 2026

What the new standards might look like

The NMC has not published the draft Code yet — that comes during the consultation from September to December 2026. But based on the evidence review findings, we can expect standards that cover:

  • Clinical judgment. AI tools support decision-making but do not replace it. The nurse remains accountable for their clinical decisions.
  • Data protection. Using AI tools that process patient data must comply with confidentiality and data security requirements.
  • Competence. Professionals must understand the tools they use, including their limitations and potential for error.
  • Transparency. Patients should know when AI is being used in their care.

How this applies to revalidation

Technology is already part of revalidation. Many nurses use digital tools to log CPD, write reflective accounts, and organise their portfolios. The new Code standards will reinforce that this is acceptable and expected, as long as it is done responsibly.

AI tools that help you reflect on your practice, organise your learning, or track your hours are likely to be seen as supporting professional development rather than replacing it. The key is that you remain in control of your portfolio and your reflection is genuinely your own.

What the public told the NMC

The public consultation findings published in the Council papers reveal that patients and the public are broadly positive about technology in healthcare, but they want safeguards. They want to know how their data is used, who is accountable when technology gets it wrong, and they want the human element of care preserved.

The NMC's proposed standards on digital technology and AI are a direct response to these public concerns.
