Your Confirmer Just Changed: What the NMC's New Rules Mean

Published 23 July 2026 · Updated 23 July 2026

If you thought you could pick anyone as your revalidation confirmer, the NMC is changing that. Under proposed rules announced on 21 July 2026, your line manager becomes the default confirmer. If you want someone else, you will need to justify it.

The change was reported by Nursing Times, which quoted directly from the NMC Council papers: "We will set out more clearly that confirmers should usually be line managers by asking registrants proposing a different confirmer to tick a box from a menu of authorised departures."

Key change: Line managers become the default confirmer. Deviating from this requires selecting from a menu of authorised reasons. This is designed to strengthen the accountability link between revalidation and practice.

Who can be a confirmer under the new rules

The current rules are relatively flexible. Your confirmer must be on the same part of the register as you, or a registered healthcare professional who has been on a register for at least three years. They must also hold a letter of good standing or equivalent. They do not need to be your line manager.

The new proposals shift this. The NMC wants confirmers to usually be line managers because they have direct oversight of your practice. This makes sense: your line manager sees your day-to-day work, knows your development areas, and can genuinely confirm that you have met the standards.

If you want a different confirmer — for example, a colleague who moved to a different organisation but knows your work well — you will select from a menu of authorised departures. This replaces the current informal approach.

What counts as an authorised departure

The NMC has not yet published the full list of authorised departure reasons. That will come during the public consultation from September to December 2026. Based on the Council papers, likely categories include:

  • Working for an agency or bank without a consistent line manager.
  • Self-employment or independent practice.
  • Working in a setting where the line manager is not a registered professional.
  • A recent change of employer that means your new line manager does not know your practice well enough.
  • Other circumstances the NMC deems valid.

Source: Nursing Times report on NMC Council papers

How to prepare

The changes are expected from April 2028, so you have time. But there are things you can start doing now:

Know who your confirmer will be. If you plan to use your line manager, have the conversation early. Make sure they understand the confirmer role and what it involves.

If you need a different confirmer, document why. Start thinking about which authorised departure category applies to you. The NMC will likely expect a clear rationale, so have your reasons ready.

Build your reflective summary. Regardless of who confirms you, the reflective discussion and confirmation rely on a clear, well-documented portfolio. Your reflective accounts, CPD log, and practice hours need to be organised.
